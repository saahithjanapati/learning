# Biology Course Overview

Source note: Grounded in the College Board 2025-26 biology course framework, Khan Academy's college biology unit map, and OpenStax Biology 2e. Local course label: Biology.

## How To Read This Course

Use this note as the map before the territory.

Biology can look like a pile of disconnected facts: water is polar, mitochondria make ATP, Mendel had peas, enzymes denature, ecosystems collapse, DNA is transcribed, allele frequencies change. The trick is to stop treating those as separate trivia cards. They are all versions of the same question:

How do living systems maintain organized, adaptive behavior in a changing world?

The course answers that question at different scales. Molecules have shapes and charges. Cells use membranes and compartments. Organisms regulate signals and reproduce. Populations evolve. Ecosystems move energy and matter through networks of interacting species.

## The Four Big Threads

### Energy

Life is not magic. It is expensive chemistry.

Cells constantly fight the drift toward disorder. They build molecules, pump ions, copy DNA, move materials, repair damage, and divide. All of that requires energy. The energy thread starts with chemical bonds and ATP, passes through enzymes, photosynthesis, and respiration, and later reappears in food webs and ecosystem energy flow.

When a biology question feels fuzzy, ask: Where is the energy coming from, where is it going, and what work is being powered?

### Information

Life stores instructions and changes them over time.

DNA is not a tiny blueprint in the simple sense. It is more like a molecular archive that cells read, copy, edit, regulate, and sometimes miscopy. Information flows from DNA to RNA to protein, but it is also shaped by gene regulation, cell specialization, mutation, inheritance, and selection.

When you study heredity or gene expression, ask: What information is being transmitted, read, changed, or regulated?

### Structure And Function

Biological form is usually functional, and function usually depends on form.

Water's polarity explains its solvent behavior. Protein shape explains enzyme specificity. Membrane phospholipids explain selective barriers. Large cell size creates surface-area problems. Chromosome behavior explains inheritance patterns. A food web's structure shapes ecosystem stability.

When you meet a new structure, ask: What problem does this structure solve?

### Interactions

Biology happens in relationships.

Atoms bond. Proteins bind. Membranes separate and exchange. Cells signal. Genes interact with environments. Predators, prey, competitors, mutualists, and decomposers shape communities. Natural selection itself is an interaction between variation and environment.

When a process feels abstract, ask: Which parts are interacting, and what changes because of that interaction?

## The Course Arc

### 1. Chemistry of Life

This unit explains why life uses the molecules it uses. Water is not just background liquid; its polarity and hydrogen bonding make cells possible. Carbon is not just another element; its bonding flexibility lets it form chains, rings, and functional groups. Macromolecules are not random large molecules; their structures let cells store energy, build membranes, catalyze reactions, and encode information.

The central idea: molecular structure creates biological possibility.

### 2. Cells And Membranes

Cells are bounded systems. They need to keep some things in, keep some things out, and move selected materials across the border. They also need enough internal organization that chemical reactions do not become chaos.

Membranes, organelles, transport proteins, gradients, tonicity, and compartmentalization are all ways cells manage boundaries.

The central idea: cells survive by controlling internal conditions while exchanging matter and information with the outside.

### 3. Cellular Energetics

Enzymes make reactions usable at biological temperatures. ATP lets energy-releasing reactions power energy-requiring reactions. Photosynthesis captures light energy into chemical form. Cellular respiration harvests chemical energy into ATP.

The central idea: living systems do not create energy; they transform and manage it.

### 4. Cell Communication And Division

Cells need to know what is happening around them and what state they are in. Signals bind receptors, pathways relay information, feedback stabilizes or amplifies responses, and checkpoints decide whether division should proceed.

The central idea: multicellular life depends on controlled communication and controlled growth.

### 5. Heredity

Inheritance is not only "traits from parents." It is chromosome behavior plus probability. Meiosis shuffles genetic information. Fertilization combines it. Mendelian patterns are the clean starting cases; non-Mendelian patterns show what happens when genes are linked, sex-linked, polygenic, extranuclear, or shaped by environment.

The central idea: heredity is the transmission of information through cells, chromosomes, and chance.

### 6. Gene Expression And Biotechnology

DNA stores information, but cells must use it. Replication copies DNA. Transcription makes RNA. Translation builds proteins. Regulation determines when, where, and how strongly genes are expressed. Mutations change sequence; biotechnology lets humans read, copy, edit, and compare genetic material.

The central idea: cells become different and do different things because they regulate information.

### 7. Evolution And Natural Selection

Evolution is change in populations over generations. Natural selection is one mechanism: heritable variation affects survival or reproduction, so some variants become more common. But drift, gene flow, mutation, sexual selection, artificial selection, and speciation also matter.

The central idea: evolution explains both the unity and diversity of life.

### 8. Ecology

Ecology scales biology up. Organisms respond to environments. Populations grow and shrink. Communities form through competition, predation, mutualism, and disturbance. Energy flows through food webs. Biodiversity affects resilience. Human activity can push ecosystems away from prior states.

The central idea: organisms live inside systems, and system behavior is not always obvious from one species alone.

## What Makes A Good Biology Explanation

A strong biology explanation usually has five parts:

1. Name the structure or process.
2. Define the parts involved.
3. Trace the mechanism in order.
4. Connect the mechanism to a function or consequence.
5. Say what would happen if one part changed.

For example, "enzymes lower activation energy" is correct but thin. A better explanation says: an enzyme's active site binds substrates in a way that stabilizes the transition state, making the reaction more likely to occur under cellular conditions; if pH changes the enzyme's shape, substrate binding and catalysis can fail.

## How To Study This Casually

Read one lesson at a time. Do not try to memorize every term on the first pass. Biology sticks better when you keep a living map:

- molecule -> cell -> organism -> population -> ecosystem,
- structure -> function,
- energy -> work,
- information -> regulation,
- variation -> selection,
- interaction -> system behavior.

After each lesson, close the file and explain the unit in five sentences. If you can do that, the details have somewhere to attach.
