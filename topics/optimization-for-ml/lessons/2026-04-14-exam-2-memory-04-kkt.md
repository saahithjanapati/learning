## Core KKT System
For
$$
\min_x f(x) \quad \text{s.t. } h_i(x)\le 0,\; \ell_j(x)=0,
$$
with convex $f,h_i$ and affine $\ell_j$:

- Primal feasibility: $h_i(x^*)\le 0$, $\ell_j(x^*)=0$.
- Dual feasibility: $\nu_i^*\ge 0$.
- Complementary slackness: $\nu_i^* h_i(x^*)=0$.
- Stationarity:
$$
\nabla f(x^*)+\sum_i \nu_i^*\nabla h_i(x^*)+\sum_j u_j^*\nabla \ell_j(x^*)=0.
$$

## Lagrangian Form
- The Lagrangian is
$$
L(x,\nu,u)=f(x)+\sum_i \nu_i h_i(x)+\sum_j u_j \ell_j(x).
$$
- Equality multipliers are free in sign.
- Inequality multipliers must satisfy $\nu_i\ge 0$.
- Everything in KKT comes from this Lagrangian.

## Sufficiency / Necessity
- Necessary means:
  - if a point is optimal, then it must satisfy the condition
  - so if KKT fails, the point cannot be optimal
- Sufficient means:
  - if a feasible point satisfies the condition, that is enough to guarantee optimality
  - so once KKT holds, you are done
- In short:
  - necessary = "must have"
  - sufficient = "enough by itself"
- In convex differentiable problems with a constraint qualification such as Slater, KKT is necessary and sufficient.
- In nonconvex problems, KKT is only a candidate optimality condition, not sufficient for global optimality.
- Exam-safe reading:
  - convex + regularity: optimizer $\Longleftrightarrow$ KKT
  - nonconvex: KKT gives candidate points, not automatic global certificates

## Slater
- Slater means there exists a strictly feasible point for the inequality constraints.
- In convex problems, Slater helps guarantee strong duality and KKT necessity.

## Interpretation
- Complementary slackness says inactive constraints cannot push back:
$$
\nu_i h_i(x^*)=0.
$$
- Important precision point:
  - if $h_i(x^*)<0$, then $\nu_i=0$
  - if $h_i(x^*)=0$, then $\nu_i$ may be zero or positive
- Stationarity says the objective gradient is balanced by the active constraint gradients.
- In duality language, KKT is the first-order algebraic shadow of the Lagrangian saddle-point picture.
- Geometric picture:
  - $-\nabla f(x^*)$ lies in the cone generated by active constraint normals.
  - This is the normal-cone way to read stationarity.

## Nonconvex Add-On
- In nonconvex problems, KKT gives candidate stationary points only.
- After solving KKT, you may need second-order checking using the Hessian of the Lagrangian
$$
\nabla_{xx}^2 L(x^*,\nu^*,u^*).
$$
- Test it on feasible / tangent directions, not arbitrary directions.
- Memory shortcut:
  - first-order KKT finds candidates
  - second-order information helps distinguish local min vs max vs saddle
- Constraint qualifications matter:
  - an optimum can fail to satisfy KKT if the constraints are singular
  - this is why Slater / regularity hypotheses matter

## Solve-and-Verify Pattern
- Write the Lagrangian.
- Write stationarity.
- Use complementary slackness to split into cases.
- Solve algebraically.
- Check primal and dual feasibility at the end.

## Likely Exam Traps
- Mixing up inequality and equality multipliers.
- Forgetting $\nu_i\ge 0$.
- Misusing complementary slackness: if $h_i(x^*)=0$, then $\nu_i^*$ may be zero or positive.
- Saying KKT always implies global optimality even in nonconvex problems.
- Writing the stationarity signs incorrectly.
- Solving stationarity and then forgetting to check feasibility.
