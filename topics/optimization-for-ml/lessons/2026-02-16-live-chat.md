That inequality is not the full definition by itself; it is a **special case / rearranged form** of the first-order convexity condition.

Full first-order convexity condition (for differentiable convex $f$):

$$
f(y) \ge f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T (y-x)
\quad \text{for all } x,y.

Now set $y = x^*$:

$$
f(x^*) \ge f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T (x^* - x).
$$

Rearrange:

$$
f(x) - f(x^*)
\le
\nabla f(x)^T (x - x^*).
$$

So yes, your line is exactly the convexity inequality evaluated at $y=x^*$.

A global supporting hyperplane is a linear function that touches $f$ at one point and stays below $f$ everywhere.

1D intuition:
- hyperplane = line

## Response 14

Yes. Here is the telescoping part slowly.

### 1) Generic telescoping identity

If you have any sequence $a_0, a_1, \dots, a_T$, then:

$$
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(a_t-a_{t+1}) = a_0-a_T.
$$

Why? Expand:

$$
(a_0-a_1)+(a_1-a_2)+\cdots+(a_{T-1}-a_T).
$$

Everything in the middle cancels ($-a_1$ with $+a_1$, etc.), leaving only:

$$
a_0-a_T.
$$

### 2) What is $a_t$ in our GD proof?

We set

$$
a_t := \|x_t-x^*\|^2.
$$

So the telescoping object is:

$$
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\left(\|x_t-x^*\|^2-\|x_{t+1}-x^*\|^2\right)
=
\|x_0-x^*\|^2-\|x_T-x^*\|^2.
$$

Since squared norms are nonnegative:

$$
\|x_0-x^*\|^2-\|x_T-x^*\|^2
\le
\|x_0-x^*\|^2.
$$

### 3) Why this is useful

In the convex-rate derivation, you get per-step bounds of form:

$$
f(x_t)-f(x^*)
\le
\frac{1}{2\eta}
\left(\|x_t-x^*\|^2-\|x_{t+1}-x^*\|^2\right)
(\text{maybe extra controlled terms}).
$$

Now sum over $t=0,\dots,T-1$:

$$
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\big(f(x_t)-f(x^*)\big)
\le
\frac{1}{2\eta}
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}
\left(\|x_t-x^*\|^2-\|x_{t+1}-x^*\|^2\right)
\cdots
$$

Use telescoping to collapse that huge sum:

$$
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\big(f(x_t)-f(x^*)\big)
\le
\frac{1}{2\eta}\left(\|x_0-x^*\|^2-\|x_T-x^*\|^2\right)+\cdots
$$

So many unknown per-step distance terms reduce to just initial/final distance.

### 4) How this gives an $O(1/T)$ flavor

After dividing by $T$, you get an average gap bounded by something like:

$$
\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\big(f(x_t)-f(x^*)\big)
\lesssim
\frac{\|x_0-x^*\|^2}{2\eta T}
\cdots
$$

That $1/T$ dependence is the core reason you see $O(1/T)$ in convex smooth GD analyses.

## Response 27

User asked:
> Can you add a summary of everything we've covered so far up to the strong convexity, strongly convex case while you get linear convergence section 3.2 in the live chat?

Assistant response:
Absolutely. Here is a consolidated summary up to and including Section 3.2.

### Big Picture (Optimization Updates)

Most methods in this unit fit:

$$
x_{t+1} = x_t + \eta_t d_t
$$

For minimization, direction is usually downhill (e.g., $d_t=-\nabla f(x_t)$ or noisy variants).

Three core knobs we discussed:
1. direction term
2. step size
3. feasibility handling (projection vs no projection)

---

### Gradient Descent Basics

For GD:

$$
x_{t+1}=x_t-\eta_t\nabla f(x_t)
$$

1D example $f(x)=\frac12 x^2$ gives:

$$
x_{t+1}=(1-\eta)x_t
$$

So:
- if $0<\eta<2$, iterates shrink toward $0$
- if $\eta$ is too large (e.g., $3$), you get oscillatory divergence

---

### Smoothness Section (Key Derivation)

$L$-smoothness gives an upper quadratic bound:
$$
f(y)\le f(x)+\nabla f(x)^T(y-x)+\frac{L}{2}\|y-x\|^2
$$

Plugging GD step $y=x-\eta\nabla f(x)$ gives:
$$
f(x_{t+1})
\le
f(x_t)-\left(\eta-\frac{L\eta^2}{2}\right)\|\nabla f(x_t)\|^2
$$

Meaning: descent is guaranteed when coefficient is positive:

$$
0<\eta<\frac{2}{L}
$$

(and $\eta\le 1/L$ is a common conservative choice).

---

### Convex Case (Section 3.1)

Key convexity inequality used:

$$
f(x)-f(x^*)\le \nabla f(x)^T(x-x^*)
$$

This comes from supporting-hyperplane view of convex functions.

Then combine with squared-distance expansion and sum over $t$.

Core algebra mechanism: **telescoping**

$$
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(a_t-a_{t+1})=a_0-a_T
$$

with $a_t=\|x_t-x^*\|^2$.

This collapses a long sum to endpoint terms.

What we bounded in this section:
- average suboptimality gap
- best-iterate gap (via $\min \le \text{average}$)

Rate shape obtained:

$$
O\left(\frac{1}{T}\right)
$$

Important nuance: this does **not** automatically give a pointwise bound for every iterate / last iterate.

---

### Strong Convexity + Linear Convergence (Section 3.2)

Strong convexity adds a lower-curvature bound:

$$
f(x)-f(x^*)\ge \frac{\mu}{2}\|x-x^*\|^2
$$

Interpretation:
- smoothness ($L$): upper curvature control (not too sharp)
- strong convexity ($\mu$): lower curvature control (not too flat)

Using smoothness + strong convexity + suitable fixed step size yields contraction:

$$
\|x_{t+1}-x^*\|^2\le (1-c)\|x_t-x^*\|^2,
\quad 0<c<1
$$

Unrolling:

$$
\|x_T-x^*\|^2\le (1-c)^T\|x_0-x^*\|^2
$$

This is geometric decay, called **linear convergence** in optimization.

---

### Where We Are Now

You now have the core map:
1. smooth + convex -> sublinear $O(1/T)$ (average/best-iterate style bounds)
2. smooth + strongly convex -> geometric $(1-c)^T$ (linear convergence)

That transition is the main conceptual jump from Section 3.1 to 3.2.

> Can you explain how strong convexity gives the lower curvature inequality again?

Assistant response:
Yes. Short derivation (unconstrained, differentiable case):

Strong convexity definition says for all $x,y$:

$$
f(y) \ge f(x) + \nabla f(x)^T (y-x) + \frac{\mu}{2}\|y-x\|^2.

Now choose:

- Strong convexity means every tangent model is lifted by at least a quadratic bowl with curvature $\mu$.

d_{t+1}^2 \le q^2 d_t^2.

\|x_{t+1}-x^*\|^2 \le (1-c)\|x_t-x^*\|^2,

That 1D example is the exact intuition behind the multidimensional contraction result.

> I don't think we've covered sub-gradients yet. Can you explain that?

Assistant response:
Yes. Super simple version:

What problem are subgradients solving?
- A gradient only exists where a function is smooth.
- Many convex functions have kinks (like $|x|$), so gradient is undefined there.
- Subgradient is the replacement.

Definition (for convex $f$):
- A vector $g$ is a subgradient of $f$ at $x$ if for every $y$,
$$
f(y) \ge f(x) + g^T(y-x).
$$


Intuition:
- The line/plane with slope $g$ through $(x,f(x))$ must lie below the full function everywhere.
- So subgradients are "global underestimator slopes".

Set version:
- $\partial f(x)$ = set of all valid subgradients at $x$ (subdifferential).

Key example: $f(x)=|x|$
- If $x>0$: $\partial f(x)=\{1\}$
- If $x<0$: $\partial f(x)=\{-1\}$
- If $x=0$: $\partial f(0)=[-1,1]$

So at the kink, you have many valid slopes, not one.

Why this matters for optimization:
- Smooth convex optimum test: $\nabla f(x^*)=0$
- Non-smooth convex optimum test: $0\in\partial f(x^*)$

Algorithmically:
- Replace GD with subgradient method:

$$
x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta_t g_t, \quad g_t\in\partial f(x_t).
$$

That is the main idea.

User asked:

Assistant response:
Yes. I added more detail directly into the textbook sections:

Plain-English summary:

### 4.3 Why the constrained first-order condition works
Condition:
$$
\nabla f(x^*)^T(x-x^*) \ge 0\quad \forall x\in C.
$$

Interpretation:
- From $x^*$, every feasible move has nonnegative first-order change.
- So there is no feasible downhill direction.

Why necessary:
- If some feasible direction had negative inner product with gradient, a tiny move in that direction would decrease $f$, contradicting optimality.

Why sufficient (convex case):
- Convexity gives
  $f(x) \ge f(x^*) + \nabla f(x^*)^T(x-x^*)$.
- If inner product is always nonnegative, then $f(x)\ge f(x^*)$ for all feasible $x$.
- So $x^*$ is globally optimal over $C$.

Also important intuition:
- In constrained problems, gradient at optimum does not need to be zero.
- It can be nonzero and point normal to the boundary (normal cone view).

### 4.4 Why KKT works
KKT combines four things:
1. Primal feasibility: point satisfies constraints.
2. Dual feasibility: $\lambda_i\ge0$ for inequality constraints.
3. Stationarity: gradient of Lagrangian is zero.
4. Complementary slackness: $\lambda_i g_i(x^*)=0$.

Why this matches optimum behavior:
- Objective wants to move downhill.
- Active constraints apply opposing "forces" via their normals.
- Stationarity says these balance exactly: no feasible first-order descent remains.
- Complementary slackness says inactive constraints apply no force.

When it is exact:
- For convex problems (plus regularity like Slater), KKT is necessary and sufficient for global optimality.
