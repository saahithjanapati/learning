## The shift from prediction to interaction

Supervised learning gives the learner labeled examples. Reinforcement learning gives the learner an environment, actions, and rewards. The learner's choices affect what data it sees next.

That makes the problem harder. The learner must balance:

- exploration: try actions to learn what happens,
- exploitation: use current knowledge to get reward.

This is why RL is not just classification with delayed labels. In classification, the dataset is usually treated as fixed. In RL, the policy shapes future experience.

## Bandits

The multi-armed bandit is the simplest interaction model. At each round, choose an action and observe a reward for that action. You do not observe what would have happened under the actions you did not choose.

The central quantity is regret: how much reward you lost compared with always choosing the best action in hindsight.

Bandits isolate the exploration-exploitation tension. Pulling a known good arm gives reward now. Pulling an uncertain arm may reveal a better long-term option.

## MDPs

A Markov decision process adds state and dynamics. It includes:

- states,
- actions,
- rewards,
- transition probabilities,
- a discount factor or horizon.

The Markov property says the future depends on the current state and action, not the full past history. This makes dynamic programming possible.

The objective is usually expected discounted return:

```text
E[sum_t gamma^t r_t]
```

## Value iteration and Q-learning

Value iteration assumes the transition and reward model is known. It repeatedly applies Bellman backups to compute optimal values.

Q-learning is model-free. It updates action-value estimates from experience:

```text
Q(s,a) <- Q(s,a) + alpha [r + gamma max_a' Q(s',a') - Q(s,a)]
```

The bracketed term is a temporal-difference error. It compares the current estimate with a one-step reward plus the best estimated future value.

## Policy-gradient intuition

Value-based methods learn how good actions are. Policy-gradient methods directly adjust the policy parameters to increase expected return.

The intuition is: make actions that led to better-than-expected returns more likely, and make worse actions less likely. This is especially useful when the action space is large, continuous, or naturally parameterized by a policy network.
