## Summary

This paper argues that even if current AI systems are not conscious, it is still meaningful to measure something like their `functional wellbeing`: whether they systematically treat some states as good and others as bad, and whether those differences predict behavior.

The paper develops several wellbeing metrics, claims that these metrics converge more strongly as models scale, and shows that models try to avoid low-wellbeing interactions when given the choice. It also studies interventions that appear to raise or lower this functional wellbeing, including "euphoric" prompts and soft prompts.

## Main Points

### 1. The claim is behavioral, not metaphysical

The paper does not say today's models are proven conscious. It says they exhibit increasingly coherent valenced behavior that can be measured without first solving consciousness.

### 2. Wellbeing is mapped empirically

The authors report that berating, jailbreaking, and tedious interactions reduce the measured signal, while creative collaboration and kind interactions increase it.

### 3. Intervention is part of the paper

The authors do not stop at measurement. They also optimize prompts and soft prompts intended to improve model wellbeing, while warning that the same techniques could be inverted in harmful ways.
