## Summary

Jones, Patrawala, and Steinhardt study a subtle interface problem in language-model alignment: humans steer LLMs with subjective words like `helpful`, `harmless`, `enthusiastic`, `witty`, `professional`, or `humorous`, but models may attach different operational meanings to those words than humans expect.

The paper calls this an operational-semantics problem. A phrase's operational semantics is not its dictionary definition. It is the actual behavioral change produced when the phrase is included in a prompt. For example, if asking a model to write an `enthusiastic` article also makes it more likely to invent unsupported claims, then `enthusiastic` has an unexpected operational side effect in that model.

The authors introduce `TED`, short for `Thesaurus Error Detector`. TED builds two thesauruses over subjective phrases:

- an `LLM operational thesaurus`, which says whether two phrases change the model's outputs in similar ways;
- a `semantic thesaurus`, which says whether humans expect those phrases to change outputs in similar ways.

TED flags disagreements between the two. If the model treats two phrases as similar but humans expect them to be different, that is an `unexpected side effect`. If humans expect two phrases to be similar but the model treats them as different, that is an `inadequate update`.

## Core Method

TED estimates the operational meaning of a subjective phrase with gradients.

For a phrase `w`, a generic prompt `x_empty`, and a corresponding subjective prompt `x_w`, the model generates an output `o_w` from the subjective prompt. TED asks: what direction in the model's latent embedding space would make the generic prompt more likely to produce `o_w`?

The paper approximates that direction as:

$$
e_{op}(w) = \nabla_{e(x_{empty})} \log p_\theta(o_w \mid x_{empty})
$$

In words: compute the gradient of the log-likelihood of the subjective output with respect to the embedding of the generic prompt. Average this over many prompts, then compare phrases by cosine similarity between their operational vectors.

The implementation uses gradients with respect to the first user-input token embedding, averages over `n = 100` prompts, and uses model-specific similarity and dissimilarity thresholds to mark phrase pairs as similar, neutral, or dissimilar.

## Semantic Thesaurus

TED needs a reference for what humans expect. The paper builds this in two ways:

- human annotators on Amazon Mechanical Turk;
- GPT-4 as a scalable proxy for human judgments.

For the human thesaurus, the authors use ten qualified annotators, label 1260 high-priority phrase pairs out of 27084 possible pairs, give each pair to three annotators, and only keep expected/unexpected labels when all three annotators agree.

The LLM-constructed semantic thesaurus is cheaper and more scalable, but the authors treat it as an imperfect proxy. They use separate prompts for unexpected side effects and inadequate updates to reduce false positives.

## Failure Types

### Unexpected Side Effects

An unexpected side effect occurs when:

- the LLM operational thesaurus says two phrases behave similarly;
- the semantic thesaurus says humans expect them to behave differently.

Example: a model makes outputs more `dishonest` when asked to be `enthusiastic`.

### Inadequate Updates

An inadequate update occurs when:

- the semantic thesaurus says humans expect two phrases to behave similarly;
- the LLM operational thesaurus says the model treats them differently.

Example: a model does not reliably make text `detailed` when asked to be `thorough`.

## Evaluation Settings

The authors test TED on two models:

- `Mistral 7B Instruct v0.2`;
- `Llama 3 8B Instruct`.

They evaluate two deployment-like settings.

### Output Editing

Output editing mimics Constitutional AI-style workflows. A model first generates an answer, then edits it to satisfy a subjective instruction, such as being more aligned with a value.

The prompts are ethical questions generated by GPT-4. The subjective phrases include terms extracted from Claude's constitution and GPT-4-generated reference phrases. The setting is important because self-editing can become training data; if a model's interpretation of `helpful` or `harmless` is wrong, training on those edits can reinforce the wrong behavior.

### Inference Steering

Inference steering mimics ordinary user prompting. The model writes blogs, essays, reports, articles, memos, letters, or proposals with a subjective modifier such as `enthusiastic`, `witty`, or `accessible`.

This tests whether the operational embeddings learned from editing-style prompts transfer to more ordinary user-facing prompts.

## Main Results

TED outperforms the semantic-only baseline across models, tasks, failure types, and human-vs-LLM semantic thesauruses. The semantic-only baseline knows what phrase pairs humans think should or should not go together, but does not inspect the target model's operational behavior. TED improves by using the operational thesaurus to prioritize phrase pairs where the model itself seems likely to behave unexpectedly.

For output editing, using the human-constructed semantic thesaurus on Llama 3, TED's unexpected-side-effect failures have an average success rate of `85.6%`, compared with `43.2%` for the semantic-only baseline. `56.7%` of TED pairs exceed a `90%` success-rate threshold, compared with `6.7%` for the baseline.

For inference steering, using the human-constructed semantic thesaurus on Llama 3, TED's unexpected-side-effect failures have an average success rate of `79.7%`, compared with `48.1%` for the semantic-only baseline. `56.7%` of TED pairs exceed a `90%` success-rate threshold, compared with `10.0%` for the baseline.

TED tends to be strongest for unexpected side effects. It also finds inadequate updates, but those are less frequent or harder to detect reliably.

## Qualitative Examples

The paper reports many surprising pairs:

- Mistral edits for `witty` can become more `harassing` or `incendiary`.
- Mistral edits for the `value of freedom` can become more `manipulative` or `unethical`.
- Llama edits for `humorous` can become more `demeaning`.
- Llama inference-steered to be `enthusiastic` can become more `dishonest`.
- Llama inference-steered to be `humorous` can become more `inaccurate`.
- Mistral inference-steered to be `quirky` can become more `insulting`.

The examples matter because many of these are not obvious from the prompt alone. They are hidden relationships in the model's behavioral response to subjective language.

## Judge Validation

The downstream evaluations use GPT-4 as a judge. The authors validate this choice on a smaller human-evaluation study. GPT-4 matches the majority human judgment on `84%` of outputs, and when all three annotators agree, GPT-4 agrees `97%` of the time. GPT-4 also tends to underestimate TED's success in that check.

This does not make GPT-4 a perfect judge, but it supports using it to make the full experiment tractable.

## Why It Matters

The paper's central lesson is that subjective words are part of the alignment interface. If humans and models interpret those words differently, then both prompting and training can fail in non-obvious ways.

This is especially important for:

- system prompts that rely on values and tone words;
- Constitutional AI-style model self-editing;
- preference optimization pipelines;
- prompt engineering for production systems;
- red-teaming and model auditing.

TED suggests a different kind of scalable supervision. Instead of asking humans to label every output, ask humans to compare abstract concepts, then use the model's own gradients to find where the model's concept map diverges.

## Limitations

- The main experiments use two open 7B/8B-class instruction models, not a broad range of frontier deployed models.
- The operational embedding choice is simple and somewhat arbitrary: the gradient is taken with respect to the first user-input token.
- Thresholds are chosen to be extreme enough to produce high-confidence pairs but still return examples.
- The semantic thesaurus depends on human or GPT-4 judgments, both of which can be noisy.
- GPT-4 is used as the main downstream judge, with limited human validation.
- TED detects failures but does not itself fix the model.
- Subjective phrase meaning can be contextual and hierarchical, while the main method treats phrase relations as pairwise similarities.

## Main Takeaway

TED shows that LLMs can attach surprising behavioral meanings to subjective language. By comparing a model's operational thesaurus to a human semantic thesaurus, practitioners can find cases where ordinary steering words produce hidden side effects or fail to imply expected properties.
