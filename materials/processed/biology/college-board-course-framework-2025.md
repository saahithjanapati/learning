## Why This Source Matters

This is the official framework used here to set the boundary of the review course. The local course is named Biology, but this source supplies the familiar unit sequence, topic coverage, science practices, pacing signals, and exam-weighting priorities.

## Course Shape

The framework organizes the content into eight major units:

1. Chemistry of Life
2. Cells
3. Cellular Energetics
4. Cell Communication and Cell Cycle
5. Heredity
6. Gene Expression and Regulation
7. Natural Selection
8. Ecology

The largest emphasis areas are cellular energetics, gene expression, natural selection, and ecology. The official weighting also keeps chemistry, cells, communication, and heredity substantial enough that no unit can be treated as optional background.

## Science Practices To Build Into Lessons

The framework is not only a topic list. It expects repeated practice with:

- explaining biological concepts and processes,
- interpreting visual representations,
- asking scientific questions and evaluating methods,
- representing and describing data,
- using statistical tests and quantitative analysis,
- making evidence-based biological arguments.

For this repo, that means each lesson should do more than define terms. It should also ask: What would the data look like? What is the causal mechanism? What result would change our mind? Where could an experiment mislead us?

## Topic Coverage Used For The Biology Plan

Chemistry of life:
- water and hydrogen bonding,
- elements of life,
- biological macromolecules,
- carbohydrates, lipids, nucleic acids, and proteins.

Cells:
- cell structures and functions,
- cell size and surface area constraints,
- membrane structure, permeability, transport, facilitated diffusion, tonicity, osmoregulation,
- compartmentalization and the origin of eukaryotic compartmentalization.

Cellular energetics:
- enzyme structure and catalysis,
- environmental effects on enzymes,
- cellular energy, photosynthesis, and cellular respiration.

Cell communication and division:
- cell communication,
- signal transduction,
- feedback,
- cell cycle,
- cell-cycle regulation.

Heredity:
- meiosis,
- genetic diversity,
- Mendelian and non-Mendelian inheritance,
- environmental effects on phenotype.

Gene expression and biotechnology:
- DNA and RNA structure,
- replication,
- transcription and RNA processing,
- translation,
- gene regulation,
- cell specialization,
- mutations,
- biotechnology.

Evolution:
- natural selection,
- artificial selection,
- population genetics,
- Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium,
- evidence for evolution,
- common ancestry,
- continuing evolution,
- phylogeny,
- speciation,
- variation,
- origins of life.

Ecology:
- responses to the environment,
- energy flow,
- population ecology,
- density effects,
- community ecology,
- biodiversity,
- ecosystem disruptions.

## Translation Into Local Lessons

The course is written as a review-first sequence. It preserves the official content boundary but uses a calmer reading style:

- start each unit with the question the unit is trying to answer,
- explain mechanisms before vocabulary lists,
- connect molecules -> cells -> organisms -> ecosystems,
- add short "check yourself" prompts instead of long problem sets,
- leave room for future lessons beyond this framework.
